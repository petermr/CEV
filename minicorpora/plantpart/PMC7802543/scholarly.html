INTRODUCTION

Probiotics are live microorganisms which, when administered in adequate amounts, confer a health benefit on the host (FAO/WHO,Â 2001). The popularity of probiotics has continuously grown, and various probiotic food products have been marketed, including probiotic yogurts (Sarvari etÂ al.,Â 2014).

The probiotic yogurt, having probiotic effects, is a product with adjuvant microorganisms. There are numerous advantages associated with consuming fermented dairy products containing probiotic bacteria (Aswal etÂ al.,Â 2011). However, to deliver their health benefits, probiotics must be present in food products above a threshold level (&gt;6Â logÂ cfu/g) at the time of consumption in order to survive the passage through the upper and lower parts of the gastrointestinal (GI) tract (Marinaki etÂ al.,Â 2016). Nevertheless, during the storage of probiotic products, the survivability of these bacteria shows a decreasing trend due to several factors, for example, the low pH of fermented foods, hydrogen peroxide produced by some lactobacilli, and high oxygen content (Kim etÂ al.,Â 2019; Sarvari etÂ al.,Â 2014). The most commonly used probiotic supplements contain the species of Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium and are part of the healthy human intestinal microbiota (Nashaat ALâ€�Saadi,Â 2016).

Herbal products (spices, essential oils, and extracts) have been used as a source of functional flavoring agents (Azizkhani &amp; Parsaeimehr,Â 2018), bioactive antioxidants, and other compounds, such as phenolic compounds, and can be incorporated as nontraditional additives in fermented milk products, including yogurt (Mahmoudi etÂ al.,Â 2016). There are several studies about the health benefits of herbs, including antimicrobial, antioxidant, antiâ€�inflammatory, and anticarcinogenic properties (Azizkhani &amp; Parsaeimehr,Â 2018).

Combining probiotics with herbal products may provide further antimicrobialâ€�therapeutic properties. However, as herbs are antimicrobials, they may affect the viability of probiotic microorganisms. In vitro studies testing herbs on the growth of selected probiotics demonstrated that herbal products significantly enhance the growth of probiotics while inhibiting pathogens (Be etÂ al.,Â 2009; Sutherland etÂ al.,Â 2009). Also, the use of herbal extracts can exert a strong effect on food properties, including structural, functional, and nutritional changes in proteins. Several factors can influence the action and reaction of phenolic compounds, most notably pH, protein type and concentration, and the structure of phenolic compounds. (Ozdal etÂ al.,Â 2013). One way to enhance the viability of probiotic bacteria is the addition of medicinal plants to dairy products which, while increasing the viability of these bacteria and the shelf life of these products, does not adversely affect the organoleptic properties of the products. (Yerlikaya,Â 2014).

Ferulago angulata known as Chavir (Azarbani etÂ al.,Â 2014) is a native plant in some parts of Iran (Sodeifian etÂ al.,Â 2011). The genus Ferulago belongs to the Apiaceae family and is used in folk medicine for sedative, tonic, digestive, and antiparasitic effects (Taran etÂ al.,Â 2010). There have been some published reports on its significant antibacterial, antioxidant, and antidiabetic properties, and, traditionally, it was added to dairy and oil ghee to prevent decay as a strong preservative and to increase the shelf life of dairy products besides adding a pleasant taste to them (Alizadeh etÂ al.,Â 2019; Azarbani etÂ al.,Â 2014).

This study aimed to investigate the effect of the ethanolic extract and essential oil of F. angulata on the protein, physicochemical, microbial, and sensory properties of probiotic yogurt and the viability of probiotic bacteria during storage time.

MATERIALS AND METHODS
2.1
Materials and bacterial strains

Bacillus subtilis (PTCC 1720), Staphylococcus aureus (PTCC 1112), and Escherichia coli (PTCC 1330) were collected from the Industrial Microorganism Collection Center (Iran). The yogurt starter culture (express 0/1 included Streptococcus thermophilus and Lactobacillus delbrueckii ssp. bulgaricus) and probiotic bacteria (Lactobacillus acidophilus (LAâ€�5) and Bifidobacterium bifidum (Bbâ€�12)) were obtained from Chr. 2.2

The aerial parts of F. angulata subsp. were collected in July 2019 from Rayen Mountains, Kerman Province, Iran. The plant was identified and authenticated by the Agricultural Research and Promotion Center of Kerman.

2.3

To extract the essential oil (EO), the aerial parts F. angulata were airâ€�dried at ambient temperature in the shade; 150Â g of them was distilled by a Clevengerâ€�type apparatus for 3Â hr, FAEO was extracted and dried over anhydrous sodium sulfate, and stored at 4Â°C until analysis (Javidnia etÂ al.,Â 2006).

To extract the ethanolic extract (EE), the airâ€�dried parts of F. angulata were pulverized into the powdered form. The dried powder (30Â g) was extracted by the maceration method with ethanol (EtOH), and separately at room temperature, and the solvents from the combined extracts were evaporated by the rotary system (Mottaghipisheh etÂ al.,Â 2014).

2.4
Analysis method

To analyze the components of FAEE and FAEO, a gas chromatograph (Agilent 7890A) coupled with mass spectrometry (Agilent 5975 C) GC/MS (HPâ€�5MS) equipped with a column (30Â m in length, with an internal diameter of 0.25Â mm and a thin layer thickness of 0.25Â Âµm) was employed. The extract constituents were identified by the comparison of their retention indices relative to (C7â€�C20) nâ€�alkanes and by comparison of their mass spectra with those of the internal reference mass spectra library (NIST and Wiley). 2.5
Disk diffusion antibacterial activity

The antibacterial activity of the samples was evaluated against Bacillus subtilis, Staphylococcus aureus, and Escherichia coli by the disk diffusion method (Azarbani etÂ al.,Â 2014). For this purpose, the agar diffusion method was used. The bacteria were cultured for 24Â hr on the Mueller Hinton agar, and a suspension was prepared in 0.5 McFarland dilution (OD625 nmÂ =Â 0.1) in the Mueller Hinton broth. Then, 5Â ml of each bacterial suspension was cultured with the spread plate method using a sterile swap, and blank disks containing 2,560Â Î¼g/ml of each EE/EO diluted with DMSO were placed on the culture medium. Subsequently, the inhibitory zone diameter was measured after 24Â hr of incubation at 37Â°C. A tetracycline disk was used as the control disk (Moghtader etÂ al.,Â 2013).

2.6
Minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC) and minimum bactericidal concentration (MBC) test

The MIC and MBC test of FAEE and FAEO was determined by the tube dilution method described by Tabatabaei Yazdi etÂ al.Â (2014) with some modifications. The bacterial suspensions were prepared the same as the disk diffusion test. Briefly, the bacterial dilation was prepared in nine sterile tubes. Eight tubes were used for serial dilution and one for control. All the bacteria were incubated at 37Â°C for 48Â hr. After incubation, the tubes were examined for turbidity caused by the growth of inoculated microorganisms. All tubes with no growth were sampled and cultured to determine MBC. The tubes containing the lowest concentrations of FAEE and FAEO and in which no growth was observed in the relevant plate were considered as MBC (Tabatabaei Yazdi etÂ al.,Â 2014).

2.7
Yogurt preparation

Milk was mixed with skim milk powder (2%) and milk protein concentrate (0.5%) to standardize the fat (3.2%) and protein content to the desired level (10.5 w/w; Lee &amp; Lucey,Â 2010). It was pasteurized at 90Â°C for 5Â min and cooled to 43Â°C. Then, the yogurt starter (0.6 gr) and probiotic bacteria (0.4 gr) were added to milk (800 gr; EL Omari etÂ al.,Â 2020). To prepare the herbal probiotic yogurt, different concentrations of FAEE and FAEO were added to the milk (0.2% and 0.4% for FAEE, 0.01% and 0.03% for FAEO) in 100Â g packages and then incubated at 43Â°C until reaching the pH value of 4.6. They were subsequently cooled down until 4Â°C (Sadeghi etÂ al.,Â 2017). The probiotic yogurt without FAEE and FAEO was selected as the control sample. After the production of probiotic yogurts, the samples were stored at the refrigerator temperature and analyzed during storage for 21Â days (with four intervals; Ertem &amp; CakmakcÄ±, 2018; Simon etÂ al.,Â 2018).
